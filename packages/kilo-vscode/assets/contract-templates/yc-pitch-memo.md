## Style notes for this template (delete before sending)

### What "great" looks like

- The reader could repeat the *one-liner* (top of doc) to a colleague after one read.
- Section 1 (Problem) opens with a single named human in a single named situation, not a market trend.
- Section 3 (Why now) cites a *specific* date or product release that changed the landscape, not a vague "AI is transforming…".
- Section 5 (Traction) leads with a single number, and that number is monotonically true (e.g., MoM growth across the last N months, not a one-off spike).
- Section 8 (Team) avoids any sentence that could appear on a LinkedIn profile.

### Common failure modes

- **Adjective creep**: replacing nouns with adjectives ("revolutionary platform" instead of "marketplace for X"). Cut every adjective and re-read.
- **Total Available Market top-down**: claiming a $400B TAM by citing IDC. Investors read this as a yellow flag. Bottom-up wins.
- **Solution-first**: starting with what we built rather than the pain we relieve. If the Solution section makes sense before the Problem section, you have not done the customer work.
- **Manufactured urgency**: a Why-now that boils down to "the team is excited." If the same memo could have been written 18 months ago, you do not have a why-now.
- **Hidden non-goals**: pretending the product does ten things equally well. Investors infer from the omissions; make the omissions explicit in §6 (Business model) or §9 (Ask).

### How AI fills should be approached

When an `<!-- ai-fill -->` marker appears, the model is asked to:
1. Read all preceding sections of *this same memo* before generating, so the
   voice and named entities (company, founder, beachhead) stay consistent.
2. Cite a number or a named entity (a person, a product, a date) in every
   sentence that makes a claim. Generic claims should be flagged for the
   author to either substantiate or remove.
3. When unsure, prefer *less* — a shorter section that the founder can defend
   end-to-end is better than a longer section larded with weasel words.

### Reviewer rubric (cut before sending)

- [ ] One-liner is repeatable in one read.
- [ ] Problem opens with a concrete vignette and a number.
- [ ] Why-now cites a specific external trigger (not just "AI is hot").
- [ ] Market is bottom-up with the math shown.
- [ ] Traction leads with a monotonic, attestable metric.
- [ ] Competition table includes 4-6 real competitors and a "where they fail" column.
- [ ] Team paragraphs avoid LinkedIn boilerplate.
- [ ] Ask names the next-round milestones, not just the round size.

### Worked-example one-liners

To anchor the shape, here are one-liners that pass the YC sniff test
(generic shape: a verb the customer can do, ending with a measurable
constraint that the status quo cannot match):

> - "Stripe for first-mile shipping documentation."
> - "Replace your three procurement tools with a 90-second AI-drafted PO."
> - "Send instant FedNow payments from any small-business bank account."
> - "GitHub Issues for restaurant kitchens; chefs collaborate on prep specs."
> - "Generate a SOC 2-ready privacy policy in 10 minutes from your data flows."

And a few that *fail* the test, with the failure mode named:

> - "Revolutionary AI-powered platform for the modern enterprise." (zero verbs the customer does, three adjectives)
> - "We are building the future of work." (no customer, no action)
> - "An LLM-native CRM for B2B." (technology-first, no observable benefit)
> - "The Uber of X." (lazy mapping; only works when the analogy actually maps)

### What a YC partner is listening for

In the 10-minute meeting, partners are pattern-matching against four signals:

1. **Insight**: a non-obvious truth about the customer or market that you've earned through work, not by reading. The YC interview is largely about whether you have one.
2. **Velocity**: how fast you build, ship, and learn — surfaced through the Traction section's *trend*, not its absolute.
3. **Founder fit**: a one-sentence answer to "why these humans, why this company". The Team section either delivers or doesn't.
4. **Tractability of the next 18 months**: the Ask section's three milestones must be a believable plan, not a wish list.

The memo is the artefact that makes those signals legible *before* the meeting.
